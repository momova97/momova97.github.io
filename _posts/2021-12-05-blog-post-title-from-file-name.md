## some problems are better to be left unsolved

"If I had an hour to solve an issue, I'd spend 55 minutes thinking about the problem and five minutes thinking about solutions," Einstein says. This remark elucidates how geniuses such as Einstein solve challenges.

Wise people do not rush to the answer; they do not open the answer sheet and begin answering straight away; instead, they take their time. They investigate the issue; they comprehend and comprehend the problem's meaning. Before they start working on a solution, they question themselves why this problem occurs in the first place.

This method appeals to me because addressing the problem is not the first step, unlike what we were taught. Even the theme of this debate that I'm making is centered on how to solve insoluble problems rather than the problem itself.

Now that we've established how a brilliant mind like Einstein approaches an issue let's apply the principle to data scientists' daily struggles with insoluble problems. Consider a non-linear multi-objective optimization problem, for example. We usually begin by framing the problem, making variable transitions, programming the situation, obtaining an approximate answer, and ultimately, writing a report about our findings, complete with a section requesting additional research, and that's it.

Moreover, I'd want to point out that Einstein said, "We cannot solve our problems with the same mentality we used to create them." We identify problems as intractable as data scientists because we are the ones who describe the situation in the first place. Collaborating with others on such issues is usually a good idea.
